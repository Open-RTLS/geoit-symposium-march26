# Slide Content

Use this skill when the task is primarily about slide wording rather than layout, styling, or deployment.

## Goals

- Make slides fast to scan
- Keep the message obvious within a few seconds
- Turn dense ideas into presentation-first copy

## Default approach

1. Start with the one point the audience should remember from the slide.
2. Reduce copy until only the essentials remain.
3. Prefer short sentences, fragments, or noun phrases over full prose.
4. Use blue emphasis for key essentials, but cap it at 2 or 3 items per slide.
5. Keep the slide readable at presentation distance.

## Writing guidelines

- Use short sentences or phrases. Complete sentences are optional.
- Keep each slide focused on one idea or one comparison.
- Prefer 3 to 5 concise bullets over dense lists.
- Avoid paragraphs unless the user explicitly wants speaker-note-style copy.
- Lead with the conclusion, not the setup.
- Make titles do real work. Prefer a claim or takeaway over a vague topic label.
- Keep wording concrete. Favor specific nouns, numbers, and outcomes over abstract language.
- If a detail is not needed for the audience to follow the story, cut it.

## Emphasis guidelines

- Use blue emphasis only for the key essentials the audience should notice first.
- Limit blue emphasis to at most 2 or 3 items on a slide.
- Emphasize the most important word or short phrase, not whole bullet sentences.
- Do not use emphasis on every bullet; if everything is highlighted, nothing stands out.

## Reveal-specific guidance

- Write for slides, not documents.
- Assume the presenter will add detail verbally.
- When a slide starts feeling crowded, split it into two slides instead of shrinking the message into smaller text.
- If a slide mixes strategy, technical detail, and examples, separate those into clearer beats.

## Tone

- Direct
- Crisp
- Technical but plain-language
- Confident without hype

## Boundaries

- Do not change the visual theme here; use `reveal-deck` for styling or structural deck work.
- Keep this skill focused on wording, emphasis, and slide-level clarity rather than presentation-specific subject matter.
